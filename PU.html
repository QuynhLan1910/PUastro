About the Symposium

The Phenikaa Symposium on High-Frequency Gravitational Waves is a premier gathering for physicists, astronomers, and engineers to advance our understanding of extreme astrophysics through kHz gravitational waves. These elusive signals—peaking at 1–10 kHz—reveal the secrets of neutron star interiors, equation-of-state physics, and multimessenger events.

Building on the momentum from PIPC2025 and recent KAGRA/LIGO detections, the event will feature invited talks, poster sessions, and workshops on detection technologies (e.g., NEMO, ET) and simulations. Expect 100+ attendees from Asia, Europe, and the US.

Organized by Phenikaa University in collaboration with the Institute for Cosmic Ray Research (ICRR), University of Tokyo.

Featured Speaker: Prof. Takaaki Kajita

Takaaki Kajita

Director, Institute for Cosmic Ray Research (ICRR), University of Tokyo; 2015 Nobel Laureate in Physics

Prof. Takaaki Kajita is a visionary physicist whose groundbreaking discovery of neutrino oscillations—announced in 1998 using the Super-Kamiokande detector—earned him the 2015 Nobel Prize in Physics (shared with Arthur B. McDonald). This work confirmed that neutrinos have mass, fundamentally altering models of particle physics, cosmology, and the Standard Model.

Since 2008, as Director of ICRR, Prof. Kajita has pivoted to gravitational wave astronomy, championing Japan's entry into the field. As Principal Investigator for the KAGRA Observatory—a 3 km underground interferometer operational since 2020—he has overseen its integration into the global LVK network, boosting sensitivity to binary neutron star mergers and their high-frequency post-merger "ringdown" signals. KAGRA's cryogenic design enhances kHz-band detection, enabling multimessenger synergies with neutrino observatories like Super-Kamiokande.

Prof. Kajita's talk will illuminate the intersection of neutrino and GW research: "Bridging Neutrinos and kHz Gravitational Waves: Multimessenger Probes of Extreme Matter."
